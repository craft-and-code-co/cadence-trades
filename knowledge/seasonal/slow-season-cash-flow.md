# Managing Cash Flow During the Slow Season

Every trades business has a slow season, and it will test your business every single year if you're not prepared. HVAC companies die in spring and fall. Plumbers slow down in summer. Electricians see dips in winter (unless they do holiday lighting). The companies that survive and thrive are the ones that plan for the valley while they're on the peak.

## Cash Reserve Strategy

**The rule:** Maintain 2-3 months of operating expenses in a dedicated business savings account. Not your checking account — a separate savings account you don't touch except during planned slow periods.

### How to Calculate Your Reserve Target

Add up your fixed monthly expenses:
- Payroll (including your salary): $__________
- Rent/mortgage: $__________
- Vehicle payments: $__________
- Insurance: $__________
- Software/subscriptions: $__________
- Loan payments: $__________
- Utilities: $__________
- **Total monthly fixed costs:** $__________

Multiply by 2.5. That's your reserve target.

**Example:** A 5-tech HVAC company with $85,000/month in fixed costs needs $212,500 in reserves. That sounds like a lot — and it is. But losing one tech because you couldn't make payroll in October costs you $100,000+ in annual revenue. The reserve is insurance.

### How to Build Your Reserve

During peak season, set aside 10-15% of revenue into the reserve account. Treat it like a bill — it gets paid first, not last.

**Example:** If July revenue is $180,000, transfer $18,000-27,000 to reserves. Do this for 4-5 peak months and your reserve is funded.

**Rule:** Do not raid the reserve for equipment purchases, owner draws, or "investments." It's for payroll and fixed costs during slow months. Period.

## Pre-Selling Maintenance to Fill the Slow Season

Your maintenance membership program is your primary slow-season weapon. Schedule all maintenance visits during your shoulder months.

**HVAC scheduling strategy:**
- Spring AC tune-ups: March through May (before the heat hits)
- Fall furnace tune-ups: September through November
- This puts guaranteed, pre-paid work on your calendar during your two slowest periods

**Plumbing scheduling strategy:**
- Water heater flushes: October through February (before winter stress)
- Whole-home inspections: Schedule year-round but prioritize slow months

If you have 500 active memberships at 2 visits each, that's 1,000 maintenance calls to fill your slow season. At 1.25 hours per visit, that's 1,250 hours of productive tech time — roughly 6-7 weeks of work for a 5-tech team. That's the difference between laying off techs and keeping your crew intact.

## Seasonal Financing and Payment Options

Offer financing on larger jobs during slow season to keep work flowing.

**Consumer financing programs:**
- GreenSky, Synchrony, or Service Finance — these let customers finance repairs and installations with monthly payments
- You get paid in full within 2-5 business days
- The customer gets affordable monthly payments
- Typical dealer fee: 3-9% of the financed amount (built into your pricing)

**Slow-season financing promotions:**
- "0% interest for 18 months on any repair over $1,000" (March-April for HVAC)
- "No payments for 90 days on water heater replacements" (January-February for plumbing)

These promotions pull forward demand that would otherwise happen later, filling your schedule when you need it most.

## Other Slow-Season Revenue Strategies

**Duct cleaning push.** If you're in HVAC, duct cleaning is a high-margin add-on that can be done year-round. Run a campaign in your slowest month: "$299 whole-home duct cleaning." Cost to deliver: $80-120. That's strong margin work that keeps techs busy.

**Water heater replacements.** Proactive outreach to customers with water heaters over 8 years old: "Your water heater is [X] years old. The average lifespan is 8-12 years. Let's replace it on your schedule instead of during an emergency. This month, we're offering $200 off installation."

**Indoor air quality.** UV lights, air purifiers, humidifiers — these are add-ons you can sell to existing customers during maintenance visits or through targeted email campaigns.

**Commercial maintenance.** If you're residential-only, consider adding light commercial work. Small businesses, churches, and property management companies need the same services and often schedule maintenance during off-peak residential months.

## The Cash Flow Calendar

Build a 12-month cash flow projection that shows expected revenue and expenses by month. Use your last 2-3 years of data to identify patterns.

| Month | Expected Revenue | Fixed Costs | Variable Costs | Net Cash Flow |
|-------|-----------------|-------------|----------------|---------------|
| Jan | $45,000 | $85,000 | $15,000 | -$55,000 |
| Feb | $50,000 | $85,000 | $18,000 | -$53,000 |
| ... | ... | ... | ... | ... |
| Jul | $180,000 | $85,000 | $55,000 | +$40,000 |

The months with negative cash flow tell you exactly how much reserve you need and when. The positive months tell you when to fund the reserve. No surprises, no panic — just a plan.

## The Bottom Line

Slow season doesn't have to be a crisis. It's only a crisis if you didn't plan for it. Fund your reserves during peak season, fill your slow-season calendar with maintenance visits, offer financing to pull demand forward, and diversify your revenue streams. The companies that do this don't just survive the slow season — they use it to get ahead while their competitors are scrambling.
