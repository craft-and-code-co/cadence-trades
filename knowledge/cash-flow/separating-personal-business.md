# Separating Personal and Business Finances — Owner's Pay, Entity Structure, and Bank Accounts

The number one financial mistake trades business owners make is treating the business bank account like their personal wallet. They pay themselves randomly, mix personal expenses with business expenses, and have no idea whether the business is actually profitable or just generating enough cash to fund their lifestyle. Clean separation isn't just about organization — it's about building a business that's worth something.

## Why Separation Matters

**You can't manage what you can't measure.** If your mortgage payment, your truck payment, your kid's tuition, and your material supplier all come from the same account, you have no idea what the business actually costs to run or what it actually earns.

**Taxes become a nightmare.** Your accountant bills you extra hours to untangle personal from business expenses. You miss deductions because they're buried. You risk IRS scrutiny because your records are messy.

**You can't sell or value the business.** If a buyer looks at your books and sees personal expenses running through the company, the business looks less profitable than it is — and they'll offer less. Or they won't offer at all because the financials aren't trustworthy.

**You can't get business financing.** Banks and lenders want to see clean financial statements. Co-mingled finances are a red flag that can kill a loan application.

## Setting Up Owner's Pay

You need a consistent, documented salary from your business. How you structure it depends on your entity type.

### Sole Proprietorship / Single-Member LLC

You take "owner's draws" — transfers from your business account to your personal account.

- Set a consistent draw amount — same amount, same schedule (e.g., $3,000 every two weeks)
- The draw should be based on what the business can sustainably afford, not what you want to spend
- Set aside 25-30% of your draw for self-employment taxes (15.3% SE tax + income tax)
- Make quarterly estimated tax payments

### S-Corporation (Most Common for Trades)

You pay yourself a "reasonable salary" through payroll, plus take distributions from profits.

**Reasonable salary:** What you'd pay someone else to do your job. For a trades company owner who also runs calls, this is typically $60,000-120,000/year depending on your market and company size.

**Distributions:** Profit above your salary that you can take out. Distributions aren't subject to payroll taxes (Social Security/Medicare), which is the primary tax advantage of the S-Corp.

**Example:**
- Business net profit: $200,000
- Owner salary (through payroll): $85,000
- Payroll taxes on salary: ~$13,000 (employer + employee)
- Distribution: $115,000
- Payroll taxes on distribution: $0

Compared to a sole proprietorship where the full $200,000 would be subject to self-employment tax, the S-Corp saves you roughly $10,000-17,000/year in self-employment taxes.

### When to Switch to S-Corp

General rule: when your business consistently nets $60,000+ after all expenses (including a reasonable salary for you), talk to your CPA about S-Corp election. Below that threshold, the administrative costs of running an S-Corp (payroll service, additional tax filings) may outweigh the tax savings.

## Business Bank Account Setup

### Minimum Required Accounts

1. **Business checking (operating account):** All revenue deposits here. All business expenses paid from here. This is your main operating hub.

2. **Business savings (reserves):** Your cash reserve fund. Transfer a set percentage of revenue here monthly (10-15% during peak season). Don't touch it except for planned slow-season draws or true emergencies.

3. **Tax savings account:** Set aside 20-25% of net profit monthly for tax payments. Quarterly estimated payments come from this account. Not having this account is how owners get destroyed by a $40,000 tax bill in April.

### Optional But Recommended

4. **Equipment fund:** If you're saving for a truck, a piece of equipment, or a facility, put those funds in a separate account so they don't get accidentally spent.

5. **Payroll account:** Some companies use a dedicated account that gets funded each pay period specifically for payroll. This prevents a slow week from impacting your ability to make payroll.

## Rules for Clean Separation

**Rule 1: No personal expenses on the business card.** Not groceries, not gas for your personal car, not dinner with your spouse (unless it's a documented business meal). Every personal charge creates a bookkeeping entry and muddies your financials.

**Rule 2: Pay yourself a consistent amount on a consistent schedule.** Even if you had a record month. Especially if you had a slow month. Your personal financial stability shouldn't fluctuate with every good or bad week in the business.

**Rule 3: Document every owner transaction.** Every draw, distribution, or reimbursement should have a record. Your bookkeeper should be able to tell you exactly how much the owner has taken out at any point in the year.

**Rule 4: Separate credit cards.** One business card for business expenses. One personal card for personal expenses. Never cross them.

**Rule 5: Review monthly.** Look at your owner's equity account on the balance sheet every month. If it's going negative, you're taking out more than the business is earning. That's not sustainable.

## Getting Started

If your finances are currently co-mingled, here's the cleanup plan:

1. **Open a dedicated business checking and savings account this week.** Many banks offer free business checking.
2. **Set up a consistent bi-weekly or monthly owner's draw/salary starting next pay period.**
3. **Stop using the business account for personal expenses immediately.**
4. **Open a tax savings account and start setting aside 20-25% of monthly net profit.**
5. **Talk to your CPA about entity structure** — if you're still a sole proprietor making over $60K, the S-Corp conversation could save you thousands annually.
6. **Hire a bookkeeper** if you don't have one. A good bookkeeper costs $300-800/month and saves you from financial chaos. They'll categorize expenses correctly, reconcile accounts monthly, and give you the reports you need to make informed decisions.

Clean financial separation is the difference between running a business and running a hustle. It's the foundation that everything else — pricing, hiring, growth — is built on. Get this right first.
