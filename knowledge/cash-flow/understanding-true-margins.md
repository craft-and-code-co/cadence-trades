# Understanding True Margins — Gross vs. Net, Overhead Allocation, and Target Margins by Trade

"We did $1.2 million last year" means nothing if you don't know how much of that was profit. Revenue is vanity, profit is sanity. Most trades business owners focus on the top line and ignore the numbers that actually determine whether they're building wealth or subsidizing their customers' home repairs. Let's fix that.

## Gross Margin vs. Net Margin

### Gross Margin

**Formula:** (Revenue - Direct Costs) / Revenue x 100

Gross margin tells you how much you keep after paying for the labor and materials that went directly into the job. It's your per-job profitability before overhead.

**Example:**
- Job revenue: $750
- Tech labor (burdened): $96
- Materials: $85
- Gross profit: $569
- **Gross margin: 75.9%**

### Net Margin

**Formula:** (Revenue - All Costs) / Revenue x 100

Net margin is your actual bottom-line profit after paying for everything — direct costs, overhead, office staff, insurance, rent, marketing, your salary. This is the money that's actually left over.

**Example (monthly):**
- Monthly revenue: $150,000
- Direct costs (labor + materials): $55,000
- Gross profit: $95,000 (63.3% gross margin)
- Overhead: $60,000
- Net profit: $35,000
- **Net margin: 23.3%**

### Why Both Matter

Gross margin tells you if your pricing is right. Net margin tells you if your business is healthy. You can have great gross margins and still lose money if your overhead is out of control. Or you can have mediocre gross margins and be profitable if you're running lean.

## Overhead Allocation — Where the Money Goes

Most trades business owners underestimate their overhead because they don't add it all up in one place. Here's a typical overhead breakdown for a $1.5M residential service company with 6 techs:

| Category | Monthly Cost | Annual Cost | % of Revenue |
|----------|-------------|-------------|-------------|
| Office rent | $2,500 | $30,000 | 2.0% |
| Office staff (2 CSRs + office manager) | $12,000 | $144,000 | 9.6% |
| Owner's salary | $10,000 | $120,000 | 8.0% |
| Vehicle costs (6 trucks) | $8,000 | $96,000 | 6.4% |
| Insurance (GL, WC, auto, umbrella) | $5,500 | $66,000 | 4.4% |
| Marketing | $5,000 | $60,000 | 4.0% |
| Software/technology | $2,000 | $24,000 | 1.6% |
| Accounting/legal | $1,500 | $18,000 | 1.2% |
| Utilities/phone/internet | $800 | $9,600 | 0.6% |
| Training/certifications | $500 | $6,000 | 0.4% |
| Misc/supplies | $1,200 | $14,400 | 1.0% |
| **Total Overhead** | **$49,000** | **$588,000** | **39.2%** |

That's 39.2% of revenue going to overhead. If your gross margin is 60%, you keep $900,000 after direct costs. Subtract $588,000 in overhead and your net profit is $312,000 — a 20.8% net margin. Healthy.

But if your gross margin is 50% (because your pricing is too low), you keep $750,000 after direct costs. Same overhead of $588,000 leaves you with $162,000 — a 10.8% net margin. You're surviving, not thriving.

## Target Margins by Trade

These are benchmarks for well-run residential service companies:

### HVAC

| Metric | Service/Repair | Installation | Maintenance |
|--------|---------------|-------------|-------------|
| Target gross margin | 60-70% | 38-48% | 55-65% |
| Healthy net margin | 15-22% | 8-15% | 10-18% |
| Revenue mix target | 45-55% | 30-40% | 10-20% |

HVAC installation margins are lower because equipment costs are significant. But installations generate the highest total gross profit dollars per job. A $12,000 install at 42% gross margin = $5,040 gross profit. A $450 repair at 65% = $292 gross profit. You need both.

### Plumbing

| Metric | Service/Repair | Installation/Replacement | Drain Services |
|--------|---------------|-------------------------|----------------|
| Target gross margin | 60-70% | 45-55% | 70-80% |
| Healthy net margin | 15-22% | 10-18% | 20-30% |

Drain services are plumbing's secret weapon — low material cost, fast delivery, high margin. If you're a plumbing company, invest in drain equipment and market drain services aggressively.

### Electrical

| Metric | Service/Repair | Panel/Wiring | Generator Install |
|--------|---------------|-------------|------------------|
| Target gross margin | 60-72% | 45-55% | 35-45% |
| Healthy net margin | 15-25% | 10-18% | 8-15% |

Electrical service work has the highest margins in the trades because material costs are often minimal (a $3 outlet + 30 minutes of labor = a $175 service call).

### Backflow

| Metric | Testing | Repair/Replacement |
|--------|---------|-------------------|
| Target gross margin | 70-85% | 55-65% |
| Healthy net margin | 25-40% | 15-25% |

Backflow testing is extremely high-margin work — your cost is primarily labor (30-45 minutes per test) with minimal materials. A $75-125 test with $30-40 in burdened labor cost is 55-70% gross margin. At volume, this is incredibly profitable.

## The Warning Signs

**Gross margin below 50% on service work:** Your prices are too low or your labor efficiency is poor. Reprice immediately.

**Net margin below 8%:** You're one bad month away from trouble. Either revenue needs to increase, overhead needs to decrease, or both.

**Overhead above 45% of revenue:** Your overhead is bloated. Look at staffing efficiency (too many office staff for your revenue?), vehicle costs, and marketing ROI. Cut the waste.

**Installation margin below 35%:** You're either paying too much for equipment (renegotiate with suppliers) or underpricing installs (compare to market rates).

## Improving Margins

The three levers:

1. **Raise prices** — The fastest path to better margins. A 5% price increase on the same cost base drops almost entirely to the bottom line.

2. **Reduce direct costs** — Negotiate better material pricing, improve tech efficiency, reduce callbacks. A 3% reduction in direct costs has the same effect as a 3% price increase.

3. **Control overhead** — Review every overhead line item annually. Ask: "Is this necessary? Is there a cheaper option? Is this generating a return?" Cut ruthlessly.

Track your margins monthly. Compare month-over-month and year-over-year. Set targets and hold yourself accountable. The owners who know their numbers are the owners who grow profitably.
