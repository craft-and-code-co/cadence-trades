# Job Costing Fundamentals — Know Exactly What Every Job Costs You

If you don't know what a job costs you, you don't know if you made money on it. And if you don't know which jobs make money and which ones lose money, you're guessing your way to bankruptcy. Job costing is the practice of tracking every dollar of cost against every job so you can see a per-job profit and loss statement. It's the most important financial discipline in a trades business.

## Direct Costs vs. Indirect Costs

### Direct Costs (Variable — Change With Each Job)

These are costs you can tie directly to a specific job:

**Labor:** The burdened cost of your tech's time on this job. Not their hourly wage — their fully burdened rate (see labor burden calculation). If your tech's burdened rate is $48/hr and they spent 2 hours on a job, direct labor cost is $96.

**Materials:** Every part, component, and supply used on the job. A capacitor, a faucet, a circuit breaker, solder, tape, pipe dope — all of it. Track at your cost, not retail.

**Subcontractor costs:** If you hired a crane, a concrete cutter, or any specialty sub for this job, that's a direct cost.

**Permits:** Job-specific permit fees.

**Equipment rental:** If you rented a tool or piece of equipment specifically for this job.

### Indirect Costs (Fixed Overhead — Exist Regardless of Jobs)

These costs exist whether you run 0 jobs or 50 jobs this week:

- Office rent/mortgage
- Office staff salaries (dispatchers, CSRs, bookkeeper)
- Vehicle payments and insurance (the fixed monthly cost, not fuel)
- Business insurance (general liability, E&O)
- Software subscriptions (service software, CRM, accounting)
- Marketing costs
- Loan payments
- Utilities and phone
- Insurance
- Owner's salary

Indirect costs get allocated across all jobs as an overhead rate (covered below).

## Building a Per-Job P&L

For every completed job, you should be able to produce this:

| Line Item | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| **Revenue (what the customer paid)** | $850 |
| Less: Direct labor (2 hrs x $48) | -$96 |
| Less: Materials (parts at your cost) | -$127 |
| Less: Subcontractors | -$0 |
| **= Gross Profit** | **$627** |
| **Gross Margin** | **73.8%** |
| Less: Overhead allocation | -$185 |
| **= Net Profit (this job)** | **$442** |
| **Net Margin** | **52.0%** |

That's a profitable job. But what if the tech took 4 hours instead of 2 and used $300 in materials?

| Line Item | Amount |
|-----------|--------|
| Revenue | $850 |
| Less: Direct labor (4 hrs x $48) | -$192 |
| Less: Materials | -$300 |
| **= Gross Profit** | **$358** |
| **Gross Margin** | **42.1%** |
| Less: Overhead allocation | -$185 |
| **= Net Profit** | **$173** |
| **Net Margin** | **20.4%** |

Still profitable, but barely. Now imagine the tech spent 6 hours and used $400 in materials because of an unexpected complication — that job might break even or lose money. Without job costing, you'd never know.

## Calculating Your Overhead Rate

Your overhead rate tells you how much indirect cost to allocate to each job.

### Method 1: Per-Labor-Hour Allocation

**Formula:** Total monthly overhead / Total billable labor hours per month

**Example:**
- Monthly overhead: $42,000
- 5 techs x 22 working days x 5.5 billable hours = 605 billable hours/month
- Overhead rate: $42,000 / 605 = **$69.42 per billable hour**

For a 2-hour job: overhead allocation = 2 x $69.42 = $138.84

### Method 2: Percentage of Revenue

**Formula:** Total monthly overhead / Total monthly revenue

**Example:**
- Monthly overhead: $42,000
- Monthly revenue: $150,000
- Overhead rate: 28%

For an $850 job: overhead allocation = $850 x 28% = $238

Both methods work. Pick one and use it consistently. Method 1 is more accurate for service work. Method 2 is simpler for quick estimates.

## Tracking Methods

### In Your Service Software

ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro, and Jobber all have job costing features. For each job:
- Log actual time (clock in/clock out)
- Record all materials used (from your price book)
- Assign the correct technician (for burdened labor rate)

Run a weekly job costing report. Sort by gross margin. Look for patterns: which job types are most profitable? Which techs consistently deliver higher-margin work? Which jobs are you losing money on?

### The Weekly 15-Minute Review

Every week, spend 15 minutes reviewing your job costing data:

1. What was your average gross margin this week? (Target: 55-65% for service)
2. Were there any jobs below 40% margin? Why?
3. Is there a tech who consistently has lower margins? (Training opportunity)
4. Are specific job types consistently low-margin? (Pricing problem)
5. Were any jobs estimated at 1 hour but took 3? (Diagnostic or skill issue)

## What Job Costing Reveals

**Underpriced services.** If your drain cleaning consistently shows 35% gross margin while everything else is at 55%, your drain cleaning prices are too low.

**Inefficient techs.** If Tech A averages 55% margin and Tech B averages 40% on the same job types, Tech B needs coaching on efficiency, diagnosis, or parts management.

**Material waste.** If material costs per job are creeping up, someone's not managing inventory or is ordering parts they don't use.

**Scope creep.** If a 1-hour repair keeps turning into 3-hour jobs, your diagnostic process or estimating is off.

**Your most profitable services.** Double down on marketing these. If water heater replacements are your highest-margin job, run campaigns specifically for that service.

Job costing isn't optional — it's the financial dashboard for your operation. Without it, you're driving blind. With it, you can make informed decisions about pricing, staffing, training, and marketing that compound into serious profit growth.
