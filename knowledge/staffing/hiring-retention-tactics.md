# Finding, Hiring, and Keeping Great Technicians

The trades labor shortage is real, and it's not getting better. Every owner I talk to says the same thing: "I can't find good techs." The truth is, good techs exist — they're just already employed somewhere else, and you need to give them a reason to come work for you. Here's how to find them, hire the right ones, and keep them from leaving.

## Where to Find Technicians

**Trade schools and apprenticeship programs.** Build relationships with local trade schools before students graduate. Offer to guest-lecture, sponsor a tool scholarship, or host shop tours. The companies that show up first get first pick.

**Supply houses.** Your local HVAC, plumbing, or electrical supply house sees every tech in town. Let the counter guys know you're hiring. Drop off business cards. Some supply houses will even post job flyers.

**Your competitors' unhappy techs.** Not poaching — positioning. When a competitor cuts benefits, lowers commission, or treats their people poorly, the good techs start looking. Be visible on social media, at trade events, and in the supply houses. When they're ready to move, you want to be top of mind.

**Indeed and ZipRecruiter.** Still the highest-volume sources for trades hiring. Budget $300-500/month for job postings. Write ads that focus on compensation, culture, and growth — not just "requirements."

**Social media recruiting.** Post day-in-the-life content on Facebook and Instagram. Show your trucks, your shop, your team events. Techs want to see what it's actually like to work for you. A 60-second video of your shop and team culture will outperform any job posting.

**Employee referral bonuses.** Pay your current techs $1,000-2,500 for every referral who makes it past 90 days. Your best employees know other good techs. This is the highest-quality, lowest-cost recruiting channel available.

## Interview Red Flags

Watch for these during interviews — they'll save you from bad hires:

- **Badmouths previous employers.** Everyone has had a bad boss, but a tech who spends the interview trashing their last company will do the same to you.
- **Can't explain their troubleshooting process.** Ask "Walk me through how you'd diagnose a no-cooling call." If they can't articulate a logical diagnostic flow, they're guessing in the field.
- **Job-hopped every 6-12 months.** One or two short stints happen. Five in a row is a pattern.
- **Doesn't ask any questions.** Good techs want to know about your call volume, tools provided, training opportunities, and advancement paths. A tech with zero questions isn't serious or isn't curious — both are problems.
- **Won't do a ride-along or working interview.** Top candidates are willing to prove themselves. If someone refuses a paid working interview, they're hiding something.

## What Good Techs Actually Want

It's not just about money. Surveys of trades workers consistently rank these factors:

1. **Respect and recognition** — Being treated as a professional, not a number
2. **Reliable, well-maintained equipment** — Good trucks, good tools, working inventory
3. **Competitive pay with earning potential** — Base + commission or bonuses (see performance-based pay)
4. **Schedule predictability** — Reasonable on-call rotations, realistic daily call counts
5. **Training and advancement** — Paid certifications, career paths to lead tech or supervisor
6. **Benefits** — Health insurance, retirement match, PTO. These are table stakes.

## Retention Strategies That Work

**90-day onboarding.** The first 90 days determine whether a new tech stays or leaves. Assign a mentor, check in weekly, and don't throw them into the deep end alone. Have a structured training plan for their first 12 weeks.

**Annual pay reviews.** Don't wait for a tech to threaten to leave before you adjust their pay. Review compensation annually and proactively raise top performers above market rate. It's cheaper to give a $2/hr raise than to recruit and train a replacement ($8,000-15,000 in hiring costs + lost revenue).

**Career pathing.** Show techs where they can go: Apprentice, Journeyman, Senior Tech, Lead Tech, Field Supervisor, Service Manager. Map out what skills and certifications are required at each level, and what the pay increase looks like.

**Tool and truck programs.** Provide quality tools and well-stocked, clean trucks. Letting techs take their truck home is a massive perk that costs you very little but feels huge to them.

**Team culture.** Monthly team lunches, quarterly outings, annual appreciation events. Recognize top performers publicly. Celebrate work anniversaries. These small investments build loyalty that a competitor's $1/hr raise can't break.

**Exit interviews.** When someone does leave, find out why. If three techs in a row mention the same issue (bad dispatcher, no training, unreasonable call volume), you've got a systemic problem to fix.

## The Math of Retention

Replacing a technician costs $8,000-15,000 when you factor in recruiting, onboarding, lost productivity, and the revenue gap while the position is open. If you have 8 techs and lose 3 per year (37.5% turnover — common in the trades), that's $24,000-45,000/year in replacement costs alone. Cutting turnover by just one tech per year through better culture, pay, and management saves you $8,000-15,000. Invest that money in the retention strategies above and you'll break the cycle.
