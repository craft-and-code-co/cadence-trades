# Material Markup Strategies — Standard Markups, Supplier Negotiations, and Inventory Management

Materials are a significant cost on most trades jobs — anywhere from 10% of the invoice (simple service calls) to 60%+ (equipment installations). How you price and manage materials directly impacts your profitability. Too many trades business owners just slap a flat markup on parts and hope for the best. A strategic approach to material pricing can add 5-10 points to your gross margin.

## Standard Markup Tiers

Not all parts deserve the same markup. Small, inexpensive parts should carry a higher markup percentage because the dollar amount is still small. Large, expensive equipment carries a lower percentage but a larger dollar amount.

### Tiered Markup Structure

| Material Cost | Markup | Selling Price | Rationale |
|--------------|--------|---------------|-----------|
| Under $10 | 400-500% | $40-50 | Handling cost exceeds part cost. A $2 capacitor that takes 5 minutes to source and stock costs you more in overhead than the part itself. |
| $10-$50 | 250-350% | $25-175 | Standard repair parts. Markup covers stocking, warranty, and convenience. |
| $50-$200 | 150-200% | $75-400 | Mid-range components (blower motors, faucets, breakers). |
| $200-$500 | 100-150% | $400-750 | Major components (compressors, water heaters, panels). |
| $500-$2,000 | 50-75% | $750-3,500 | Large equipment. Customer is more price-aware at this level. |
| Over $2,000 | 25-40% | $2,500-2,800+ | Major equipment (condensers, furnaces). Percentage is lower but dollar margin is significant. |

### Why Tiered Markups Work

A flat 100% markup on everything sounds simple but fails in practice:
- A $3 wire nut at 100% markup = $6 selling price. You've made $3 — not enough to cover the cost of having it on the truck.
- A $3,000 condenser at 100% markup = $6,000 selling price. Now you're $1,500 above market and the customer gets three quotes from competitors who marked it up 35%.

Tiered markups solve both problems: small parts generate enough margin to justify stocking and handling, while large equipment stays competitive.

## Flat Rate Price Book Integration

In a flat rate system, material cost is baked into the task price and the customer never sees a parts list. This is ideal because:

1. **No line-item negotiation.** The customer can't Google the part price and argue about your markup.
2. **Consistent pricing.** Every tech quotes the same price for the same repair.
3. **Margin protection.** Your markup is invisible — the customer evaluates the total price for the solution, not the parts cost.

When building flat rate prices:
- Calculate material cost for each task at your cost
- Apply the appropriate tiered markup
- Add burdened labor and overhead
- Set the final price at your target gross margin

If a capacitor costs you $12 and you mark it up to $48 (300%), then add $72 labor and $35 overhead, your total cost is $119 and you might price the task at $289 (59% gross margin). The customer sees "$289 to replace your capacitor" — a fair price for a professional repair with a warranty.

## Supplier Negotiations

Your material cost directly impacts margin. Even small savings on parts compound across thousands of jobs.

### Negotiation Levers

**Volume commitments.** Tell your supplier: "I'll commit to $X/month in purchases if you give me a better price tier." Most suppliers have volume discount brackets they don't advertise.

**Prompt payment discounts.** Many suppliers offer 2% net 10 (2% discount if you pay within 10 days). On $10,000/month in materials, that's $200/month or $2,400/year in free margin.

**Annual rebates.** Ask about end-of-year rebates based on total annual purchases. These can be 1-5% of your total spend, paid as a check or credit.

**Competitive bidding.** Get quotes from 2-3 suppliers for your top 20 most-used parts annually. Use competing prices as leverage: "Supplier B is offering this motor at $15 less. Can you match?"

**Direct manufacturer accounts.** For high-volume parts, go direct to the manufacturer instead of through a distributor. Brands like Goodman, Rheem, and Generac offer direct dealer pricing that's 10-20% below distributor cost.

### Target Savings

A well-negotiated supply chain should save you 8-15% on material costs compared to "walk-in" pricing. On $120,000/year in materials, that's $9,600-18,000 in savings — money that drops straight to your bottom line.

## Inventory Management

### What to Stock on Trucks

Every item on a truck costs you money — the purchase price, the space it takes, and the risk of obsolescence. Stock only what your techs use frequently.

**The 80/20 rule:** 20% of your parts account for 80% of your usage. Identify your top 30-40 parts by frequency and ensure those are always on every truck. Everything else is a warehouse pull or a supply house run.

**HVAC truck essentials:** Capacitors (3-5 common sizes), contactors, blower motors (2-3 common models), refrigerant (R-410A, R-22 if still servicing), filters, thermostats, hard-start kits, relays, fuses, disconnect switches.

**Plumbing truck essentials:** Supply lines, angle stops, wax rings, toilet rebuild kits, faucet cartridges (top 5 brands), drain cleaning cables, P-traps, common fittings, solder/flux, SharkBite fittings.

**Electrical truck essentials:** Breakers (top 3 panel brands), outlets (standard, GFCI, AFCI), switches (single-pole, 3-way, dimmer), wire nuts, boxes, romex (12/2, 14/2), connectors, ground rods.

### Inventory Tracking

**Minimum approach:** Weekly truck audits. A shop manager or lead tech checks each truck against a stocking list every Monday morning. Restock what's missing.

**Better approach:** Use your service software to track parts used per job. Most platforms (ServiceTitan, Housecall Pro) let techs log parts from a catalog on each invoice. This gives you usage data, cost tracking, and automatic reorder alerts.

**Best approach:** Barcode/QR scanning on every part. When a tech pulls a part from the truck, they scan it. The system adjusts inventory in real-time and generates purchase orders when stock drops below minimum levels.

### Reducing Material Waste

**Track returns.** If techs are consistently returning unused parts to the supply house, they're over-diagnosing or over-ordering. Review return patterns monthly.

**Monitor write-offs.** Parts that sit on trucks for 6+ months without being used are dead inventory. Pull them back to the warehouse or return them.

**Standardize brands.** Don't stock 5 brands of water heater or 4 brands of thermostat. Pick 1-2 preferred brands per category. This reduces inventory complexity, improves buying power, and makes your techs more efficient (they become experts on fewer products).

Good material management isn't glamorous, but a company that buys smart, marks up strategically, and controls inventory will consistently outperform a competitor who treats material pricing as an afterthought.
