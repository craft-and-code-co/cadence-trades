# How to Structure Your Price Book — Categories, Tiers, and Option Selling

A price book is the backbone of a flat rate operation. Done right, it turns every tech into a consistent, confident salesperson who presents professional options instead of scribbling estimates on the back of an invoice. Done wrong, it's a confusing mess that nobody uses. Here's how to build one that actually works.

## Organize by Category

Structure your price book the way your techs think about jobs — by system and task type.

### HVAC Example Categories
- **Diagnostic / Evaluation** (system evaluation fee, diagnostic fee)
- **Electrical Components** (capacitors, contactors, relays, circuit boards)
- **Motors** (blower motors, condenser fan motors, draft inducer motors)
- **Refrigerant** (recharge, leak search, leak repair)
- **Controls** (thermostats, zone boards, safeties)
- **Air Quality** (filters, UV lights, humidifiers, purifiers)
- **Ductwork** (sealing, modification, replacement)
- **Full System** (changeouts, installations, conversions)

### Plumbing Example Categories
- **Diagnostic / Evaluation**
- **Drain Services** (clearing, camera, jetting)
- **Fixtures** (faucets, toilets, showers, tubs)
- **Water Heaters** (repair, flush, replacement, tankless)
- **Piping** (repair, reroute, repipe)
- **Water Treatment** (softeners, filtration, RO systems)
- **Sewer** (repair, replacement, lining)

### Electrical Example Categories
- **Diagnostic / Evaluation**
- **Outlets & Switches** (GFCI, standard, dimmer, smart)
- **Panels** (breaker replacement, panel upgrade, sub-panel)
- **Lighting** (fixture install, recessed, landscape, LED conversion)
- **Wiring** (circuit addition, rewire, dedicated circuits)
- **Generators** (portable hookup, standby install, maintenance)
- **EV Chargers** (Level 2 install, panel upgrade for EV)

## The Good / Better / Best Framework

Every repair or service should have three options. This is the single most powerful change you can make to your average ticket. Companies that implement three-option presenting see a 25-40% increase in average ticket within 90 days.

### How It Works

**Good** — The minimum fix. Solves the immediate problem and nothing else. This is the "band-aid" option. Priced at your standard flat rate.

**Better** — The recommended repair. Fixes the problem plus addresses a related concern or adds a warranty upgrade. Priced 30-50% above Good.

**Best** — The premium solution. Replaces the component/system entirely, adds a maintenance plan, or bundles complementary upgrades. Priced 80-150% above Good.

### Real Example: HVAC Blower Motor Failure

| Option | Description | Price |
|--------|-------------|-------|
| **Good** | Replace blower motor, standard warranty | $489 |
| **Better** | Replace blower motor + capacitor, extended 2-year parts warranty | $649 |
| **Best** | Replace blower motor + capacitor + complete electrical safety inspection, 3-year parts & labor warranty, includes annual maintenance membership | $879 |

### Why Three Options Work

Psychology research consistently shows that when given three choices, most people pick the middle. Without options, the customer's only choice is yes or no — and "no" wins too often. With Good/Better/Best:

- 15-20% choose Good
- 50-60% choose Better
- 20-30% choose Best

Your average ticket just jumped from $489 (one option) to roughly $660 (weighted average across all three). That's a 35% increase on the same call.

## Presentation Tips for Techs

**Always present Best first.** Start with the premium option, then work down. This anchors the customer's mind at the higher price, making Better feel like a deal.

**Use a printed or tablet-based menu.** Never just rattle off numbers verbally. A visual presentation looks professional and builds trust. Your tech should show the customer all three options side by side on a tablet or laminated sheet.

**Explain the value, not just the price.** "The Better option includes a new capacitor because that's the component most likely to fail next, and it protects your investment with a 2-year warranty."

**Let the customer choose.** Never pressure. Present, explain, and ask: "Which option works best for you?" Silence after the question. Let them decide.

## Building Your Price Book: Practical Steps

1. **Start with your top 30 repairs** — the jobs you run every week
2. **Calculate cost** for each using burdened labor rate + materials + overhead
3. **Set the Good price** at your target margin (55-65% gross)
4. **Build Better** by adding the next most likely repair or a warranty upgrade
5. **Build Best** by bundling premium service, extended warranty, and/or a membership
6. **Format cleanly** — one page per repair category, clear descriptions, large font prices
7. **Load into your field software** or print laminated cards for each category
8. **Train your techs** — role-play presentations until they're comfortable
9. **Review quarterly** — adjust prices for material cost changes and add new tasks
10. **Track option selection rates** — if nobody picks Best, it's priced too high or poorly explained

## Common Price Book Mistakes

- **Too many options** — Stick to three. Four or five options cause decision paralysis.
- **Good option is too cheap** — If Good is barely profitable, you lose when 20% of customers pick it. Every option should be profitable.
- **No descriptions** — A price without context is just a number. Each option needs a 1-2 sentence description of what's included and why it matters.
- **Never updated** — A price book from 2 years ago is wrong. Material costs, labor rates, and market conditions change. Update at least twice a year.
