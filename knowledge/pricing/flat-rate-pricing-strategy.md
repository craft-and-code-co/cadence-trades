# Why Flat Rate Beats Time & Material — And How to Build Your Price Book

If you're still charging time and material (T&M), you're leaving serious money on the table and creating a terrible customer experience. Here's the truth: customers hate T&M. They feel like the meter is running, they second-guess every minute your tech spends on site, and they have zero certainty about what the final bill will be. Flat rate fixes all of that.

## Why Flat Rate Wins

**For the customer:** They know exactly what they're paying before work begins. No surprises, no anxiety, no arguments at the invoice stage. A homeowner who sees "$387 to replace your blower motor" will say yes far more often than one watching a tech rack up hours at $125/hr wondering when it'll end.

**For you:** Flat rate decouples revenue from hours. Your best tech who finishes a job in 45 minutes earns you the same as the apprentice who takes 90 minutes — but now your best tech can run more calls per day. That's a massive revenue multiplier. Companies that switch from T&M to flat rate typically see a 20-35% revenue increase within the first year.

**For your techs:** They stop being clock-watchers and start being problem-solvers. With flat rate, the tech's job is to diagnose accurately and present options — not to justify their time.

## Building Your Price Book

A price book is your menu of services with pre-calculated flat rate prices. Here's how to build one:

### Step 1: List Every Task You Perform

Start with your 50 most common repairs and services. For HVAC, that might include capacitor replacement, blower motor replacement, refrigerant recharge, thermostat installation, drain line clearing. For plumbing: faucet replacement, water heater flush, garbage disposal install, toilet rebuild, drain clearing.

### Step 2: Calculate Your True Cost Per Task

For each task, add up:
- **Labor time** (average, not best case): e.g., 1.5 hours
- **Burdened labor rate**: e.g., $45/hr (see labor burden calculation)
- **Materials cost**: e.g., $65 for a capacitor
- **Truck/overhead allocation**: e.g., $35 per call

So a capacitor replacement costs you: (1.5 x $45) + $65 + $35 = **$167.50**

### Step 3: Apply Your Markup

Your target gross margin should be 55-65% on service work. To hit 60% gross margin, use this formula:

**Selling Price = Total Cost / (1 - Target Gross Margin)**

$167.50 / (1 - 0.60) = **$418.75** — round to $419

That's your flat rate price for a capacitor replacement. The customer sees one number. Done.

### The Markup Multiplier Shortcut

For a 60% gross margin, your multiplier on total cost is 2.5x. For 55%, it's 2.22x. For 65%, it's 2.86x.

- 50% margin = 2.0x cost
- 55% margin = 2.22x cost
- 60% margin = 2.5x cost
- 65% margin = 2.86x cost

Pick your target and multiply. Don't overthink it.

## Common Objections (And Why They're Wrong)

**"My customers will think I'm too expensive."** They won't — because they're comparing your flat rate to uncertainty. A known $419 beats an unknown $300-600. Price certainty is worth a premium.

**"What if the job takes longer than expected?"** That's the cost of doing business. Your price book is built on averages. Some jobs run fast, some run long. Over hundreds of calls, it averages out — and you'll be ahead.

**"My competitors charge T&M."** Good. That makes it even easier to differentiate. You're the professional who provides upfront pricing. They're the guys who show up and start a meter.

## Getting Started

You don't need 500 tasks on day one. Start with your top 30 repairs, price them using the formula above, and train your techs to present options using the price book. Add 5-10 tasks per month until you're comprehensive. Within 6 months, you'll wonder why you ever charged T&M.
