# When to Raise Your Prices — And How to Do It Without Losing Customers

Most trades business owners wait way too long to raise prices. They're scared of losing customers, so they eat cost increases for years until their margins are paper-thin and they can't figure out why they're working 60-hour weeks but can't make payroll. Here's the rule: **if you haven't raised prices in the last 12 months, you've already given yourself a pay cut.**

## Signs It's Time to Raise Prices

**You're booking out more than 2 weeks.** If demand is outpacing your capacity, your prices are too low. Period. You should be able to serve your market within 3-5 business days for standard work. If you're booking into next month, raise prices 8-15% immediately.

**Your margins are shrinking.** If you were making 60% gross margin last year and you're at 52% this year but haven't changed your prices, inflation just ate your profit. Material costs, fuel, insurance, wages — they all went up. Your prices need to follow.

**You can't afford to hire.** If adding another tech would put you in the red, your revenue per job is too low. You need higher prices to fund growth.

**You're losing money on certain jobs.** If your job costing shows negative margins on 15-20% of your work, your price book is stale. Recalculate and reprice.

**Your competitors charge more.** If you're the cheapest option in your market and you're not doing it intentionally as a strategy, you're undervaluing your service.

**You haven't raised prices in 12+ months.** Automatic trigger. Annual increases are non-negotiable in an inflationary environment.

## How Much to Raise

- **Annual maintenance increase:** 3-5% across the board, every January or at your fiscal year start
- **Catching up after years of no increases:** 8-12% in one shot, or two increases 6 months apart
- **Demand-driven increase:** 10-20% on your most popular services
- **Material cost pass-through:** Immediate — if copper goes up 15%, your prices go up that day

A good rule of thumb: your price increase should be at least 2x the rate of inflation. If CPI is 3.5%, raise 7-8%. You're not just covering inflation — you're covering the increases in insurance, workers' comp, fuel, and wages that outpace CPI.

## How to Communicate Price Increases

### For Existing Customers

Send a letter or email 30 days before the increase takes effect:

> "Effective [date], our service rates will be adjusted to reflect increased costs of materials, insurance, and continued investment in our team's training and certification. We remain committed to providing the highest quality [HVAC/plumbing/electrical] service in [your area]. Thank you for your continued trust."

That's it. Don't apologize. Don't explain in exhaustive detail. Don't offer discounts to soften the blow. You're a professional running a business, not a charity.

### For Membership Customers

Grandfather existing members at their current rate for 90 days, then move them to the new rate at renewal. New members get the new rate immediately. This rewards loyalty without permanently discounting your revenue.

### For Your Techs

Brief your team before the increase goes live. They need to know:
- When the new prices take effect
- Why (rising costs, investment in the team)
- How to handle pushback ("Our prices reflect the quality of service and the expertise of our technicians. We stand behind every job we do.")

## What If Customers Push Back?

Some will. Expect to lose 2-5% of your customer base on a standard annual increase. That's fine. Here's the math:

If you raise prices 8% and lose 3% of your customers, you're still ahead by about 5% in total revenue — and you're doing it with fewer truck rolls and less wear on your team. The customers you lose were almost always the most price-sensitive, lowest-margin, highest-complaint customers anyway.

## The Bottom Line

Raise prices annually. Make it a calendar event. January 1 or whenever your fiscal year starts. Treat it like renewing your insurance — it's just something you do every year to keep your business healthy. The companies that grow are the ones that price for profit, not for popularity.
